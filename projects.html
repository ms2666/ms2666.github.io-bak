A Machine Learning approach to Biomarkers for mental illness classification

We used unsupervised machine learning to redefine mental illness diagnostic categories. A wide variety of datasets ranging from fMRI to gene expressions were used in this experiment. We reported much higher levels of homogenity between groups when compared to the standard DSM categorization.

Confidence estimates for deep learning

We propose some novel solutions for calibrating the probability outputs of various neural network architectures used in practice. While the neural networks talked about in the media achieve acuracies of over 90%, many problems in practice are unable to see similar results. As such, we must be able to provide a reasonable confidence estimate for every prediction. We find that as the depth of a network increases, so does overconfidence. Our solution to this problem calibrate the network so that its confidence estimates are in proportion to the validation accuracy.

Simulating abberant salience using deep convolutional networks

We use pre-trained deep convolutional neural networks to simulate abberant salience. This is the theroy behind the visual hallucinations observed in schizophrenics. We use Google’s research on visualizing the internal representations of features to create a “deep-dreaming” network. By creating a feedback loop, we show that without a regulating mechanism, the neural network visualizes objects in places they don’t exist.

Uncovering gender bias in movie reviews

We put together the largest known dataset of movie reviews annotated with reviewer gender. We used this dataset to uncover hidden biases in the language used by female and male reviewers. We also used a novel measure, coordination, on a movie scripts dataset. This measure allows us to algorithmically discover power differentials between characters in a movie based. We use this to identify strong female characters in movies and see if there is a difference between the ratings given by female and male reviewers. We find that while movies with strong female characters do not show a significant difference between males and females, females tend to give movies with weak female characters higher ratings than males on average.

Elastic net prediction of social functioning scales

We use a large fMRI connectivity dataset to help predict a variety of social functioning scales. One of the response variables used, Social Functioning Scale (SFS), is a measure of how well a patient suffering from a mental illness like schizophrenia or bipolar disorder performs in a battery of cognition tasks. We find that fMRI data can help make significant predictions, and that elastic nets with L1 regularization can help pick out regions of interest.
